Key insight

ABCard does not necessarily have a product defect — it has a communication problem. The core mechanism (save money = have a limit) is not properly understood before first use, and the product does not step in when users fail.

Why this happens — Adoption and usage barriers
① Onboarding
All the explanation is delivered at once — before any real experience

Real understanding comes from usage, not from activation. The onboarding explains the mechanism at a single point in time and does not return when the user fails. Frustration builds without explanation — 96.2% decline rate among inactive users.

5/6 sessions with expectation gap at activation
② LG Channel
The LG who encourages adoption does not know how to guide — and this has a direct consequence

64% of LGs who encouraged adoption misunderstand how the limit works, and 59% misunderstand the Caixinha. Children of LGs with incorrect understanding accumulate 58% more declines. The LG is not just a communication channel — it is an active distortion vector.

Incorrect LG → child with 18.5 declines vs. 11.7 Correct LG
③ Understanding
Product understanding and value proposition are not clear

The core product argument — credit without debt risk — is never communicated as a benefit. The onboarding delivers content without clearly articulating the value proposition: users who arrive without prior exposure decode the own-money-as-limit as a disadvantage, not as protection.

30% error or uncertainty about the Caixinha even among active users
④ Content
The success screen describes the money cycle incompletely

"Your limit will be released within 30 minutes" — true only when payment is made with external funds. For the 47% who pay using the Caixinha itself, the money does not come back. The copy does not distinguish between the two scenarios.

47% of those who pay the bill do so using the Caixinha
Research plan hypotheses — outcomes
✅
H1 — Low deposit as a test
55% of active and 57% of inactive users deposited a minimal amount just to test. It is not a lack of money — it is a lack of trust in the mechanism.
✅
H2 — Balance/limit confusion
Those who believe the limit equals their account balance accumulate 67% more declines. 20% of active users with a decline cite "money in the account, not in the Caixinha" as the reason.
✅
H3 — Expectation of conventional credit
37% of users with a decline expected a limit without depositing. The leading reason for declines in both groups. Confirmed quali and quanti.
✅
H4 — Manual deposit friction
52% of inactive users prefer Pix to avoid the extra step. The main differentiator between those who stay active and those who drop off.
✅
H5 — Limit not understood
93% of non-converted users are wrong or do not know how the limit works. Confirmed quanti — no direct verbal confirmation in sessions.
⚠️
H6 — Liquidity as a barrier in low-income segments
11.6% cite "I have no money left over to save". Small base (n=43) — plausible hypothesis, insufficient evidence to confirm.

Hypotheses on the causes of declines
✅ Research plan hypothesis — confirmed in the field
✅
✅ Hypothesis 1 — Confirmed
Low deposit as a testing behaviour, not due to lack of income
The U18 deposits a minimal amount just to validate that the product works. The product is not yet perceived as something to be adopted, but as something to be tested.
Quanti evidence: 55% of active and 57% of inactive users declared depositing a smaller amount "just to test if the card actually worked" — the dominant behaviour in both groups.

Quali evidence:
💬 "Yeah, I did it just to test. I put in R$1, then added R$4 to make R$5. I went to my gran's pharmacy, grabbed a R$2 piece of gum, held up my phone and it went through, on credit." — Davi (S1, active)
Status: Confirmed — dominant pattern in active and inactive users; the only case without a gap had learned the mechanics via TikTok.
✅
✅ Hypotheses 2 and 5 — Confirmed
Limit understanding gap: the user does not know where it is or how it works
A significant share of users do not understand that the limit depends exclusively on what is in the Caixinha — and this gap manifests in two distinct ways in the data.
Vector 1 — Location confusion: 20.4% of active users with a noticed decline report "had money in the account, but not in the Caixinha". Among active users with incorrect limit understanding, those who think "the limit is my account balance" accumulate an average of 28.9 declines vs 17.3 for those who understand correctly — 67% more.

Vector 2 — Pre-approved limit expectation: 93% of non-converted users are wrong or do not know how the limit works; among inactive users, 27%; among active users, 24%. Those who think they have a pre-approved limit accumulate an average of 35.2 declines vs 17.3 — more than double.

Status: Confirmed quantitatively — the confusion appears implicit in usage patterns; no participant verbalised it directly in sessions.
✅
✅ Hypothesis 3 — Confirmed
Expectation of conventional credit ("expected a limit without depositing")
Users expect a pre-approved limit, replicating the logic of conventional credit. For inactive users, discovering they need to save their own money is the trigger for abandonment.
Quanti evidence: 37% of active and 39% of inactive users with a noticed decline cite "I thought Nubank would give me a limit without needing to deposit" as the reason — the most prevalent finding in both groups.

Quali evidence:
💬 "I was so excited... then I checked and it's actually my own money" — Nycole (S2, inactive)
Status: Confirmed — quali + quanti convergence.
✅
✅ Hypothesis 4 — Confirmed
Manual deposit as an extra step creates friction — Pix and debit are preferred
Pix and debit require no prior action — the card does. For inactive users, this extra step is the main differentiator. For active users, a real need compensates the effort.
Quanti evidence: 52% of inactive and 39% of active users prefer Pix or debit to avoid the extra step. Only 13% of inactive and 20% of active users consider the deposit a natural part of their routine.

Quali evidence:
💬 "I prefer Pix, it is easier" — Nycole (S2, inactive)
Status: Confirmed — quanti + quali convergence.
⚠️
⚠️ Hypothesis 6 — Partially confirmed
Low-income families perceive the product as a "liquidity luxury"
The idea of leaving money "locked" as collateral may be perceived as a liquidity risk — especially in tighter income contexts where money must be available via Pix at any time.
Quanti evidence: "I have no money left over to save" appears in 11.6% of non-converted users with answered barriers — the 5th most cited barrier. (small base: n=43 non-converted users with answered barriers)

Quali evidence: The hypothesis found no direct accounts in the available qualitative sessions.

Status: Partially confirmed — present in the data as a barrier, but with a small base and no qualitative confirmation. Requires further investigation.
🔍 Emerging hypotheses — raised from this research
🧩
🔍 Central hypothesis — New
The user has a mistaken understanding of what happens to money deposited in the Caixinha
A significant share of declines occurs because the user does not understand what happens to values in the Caixinha — and therefore attempts purchases above the effective limit without realising it. The offshoots below map the specific mechanisms.
📋
📋 Misconceptions about the Card Caixinha
① Committed money as collateral
The amount used in purchases is blocked as collateral — it cannot be withdrawn or reused as a limit until the bill is paid.
Survey data: 19.9% of active and 22.1% of inactive users believe they can withdraw money from the Caixinha via Pix at any time.
② Displayed amount ≠ available limit
The Caixinha screen shows the total deposited, not the real available limit (total − committed). Someone who deposited R$200 and used R$120 still sees R$200 — and attempts purchases beyond the real limit without realising.
Interface hypothesis — no direct survey data. Validation requires contextual analysis or screen flow analytics (see Hypothesis 3 below).
③ Automatic charges reduce the limit
Recurring subscriptions (Google One, Spotify) attempt to debit the limit on the same day of the month as the initial payment, regardless of available Caixinha balance. The user does not notice because they did not initiate the transaction.
Quali evidence: Davi/S1 and S5 accumulated dozens of automatic declines without noticing. No direct survey data.
💬 Davi (S5) — the most careful participant in the field — accumulated 13 automatic Google One declines without noticing, as he did not associate them with purchases he had initiated.
④ Deposit is collateral, not a debit
The deposit is not spent at purchase — it is locked as collateral and only consumed if the bill is not paid with external funds. Those who do not understand this may reject the product as a financial risk.
Quali evidence: João Victor (S6) feared paying "twice" — the purchase and the bill — not understanding that the Caixinha amount is the collateral itself, not a separate debt.
💬 "It would be two debts" — João Victor (S6), describing the fear of paying for the purchase and then the bill
📱
🔍 Hypothesis 1 — Offshoot
Caixinha screen does not show available limit
The screen shows the total deposited as the main value. Users who deposited R$200 and used R$120 still see R$200 — and attempt purchases beyond the real limit without realising.
Quali evidence: Davi/S1 accumulated 13 automatic declines without noticing. Status: Hypothesis — requires transactional cross-check to confirm.
📊BA: Cross declines with transaction type (recurring vs. one-off)
💡 Follow-up Unmoderated test (Maze) or contextual inquiry: what does the user understand from the Caixinha screen information?
🔄
🔍 Hypothesis 2 — Offshoot
Recurring subscriptions consume the limit without the user noticing
Automatic charges (Google One, Spotify) debit from the limit on the due date, regardless of the Caixinha balance at that moment. The user does not associate these declines with the product — from their perspective, they did not initiate any purchase.
Quali evidence: Davi/S5 — 13 Google One attempts, not reported as declines since he did not initiate them. Status: Hypothesis — correlation analysis requested.
📊BA: Recurring subscriptions × declines correlation per user
🔍
🔍 Hypothesis 3 — Offshoot
User consults the Caixinha — not the Bill — to check the limit
The screen shows the total deposited — not the real available limit (total minus committed). A user who sees R$100 but has R$40 available attempts purchases beyond the limit without realising, generating unexplained declines.
Origin: Interface analysis — no participant reported consulting the bill screen before purchasing.Status: Hypothesis — requires contextual analysis or screen flow analytics (see Hypothesis 3 below).

Interface as a complementary hypothesis

A reading of the screens, cross-referenced with field accounts, suggests that interface elements may be contributing to the incorrect mental models observed. The findings below are hypotheses raised from qualitative analysis and heuristic review — these conclusions have no direct quantitative evidence, but point to paths that warrant investigation and validation.

Card Caixinha

Gross amount as the main information and "Being used as collateral" — without calculating the available limit

Finding 1 — Card Caixinha screen
The screen does not show the available limit
The prominently displayed value is the total deposited. The "being used as collateral" field appears as secondary information, without automatically translating how much limit is still available for purchases.

A user who deposited R$100 and used R$30 sees R$100 as the main number. To know they only have R$70 of limit, they need to subtract "being used as collateral" from the total — a calculation that is not prompted and that most users do not make.

Hypothesis
The user reads the main value on the screen, does not subtract what is committed, and attempts a purchase that exceeds the real limit. Consistent with field accounts, but not directly tested — requires cross-referencing declines with the caixinha state at the moment of each attempt.
Interviewees generally did not report declined purchases in volumes consistent with those recorded internally. This suggests that (1) many do not notice when a purchase is declined, especially automatic background charges — and (2) they do not always notice when they have no available balance, even customers like Davi (S5), who "always checks" the balance, yet still accumulated 13 automatic declines without noticing.
Possible impact Guided by the most prominent information on the screen, the user may be confusing the available limit with the Caixinha balance. The user is not making a mistake — the product is leading them to make one.
💡 Follow-up user research
Where does the user look for information about their limit and what do they understand from the Caixinha screen? Contextual inquiry or unmoderated testing to observe whether the user perceives the distinction between the total deposited amount and the amount effectively available as limit.
Card Bill
Finding 2 — Bill screen
The correct information is on the screen the user will not consult
The bill screen correctly displays the available limit just below the current bill. The information is there, readable, requiring no calculation.

The problem is structural: reaching this screen requires the user to think in terms of a bill — and a bill, for this audience, is synonymous with debt. Those who have not incurred debt do not open the bill screen to check how much they can spend.

The natural entry point is the caixinha: that is where the money is, where the limit originates, where the user will check "how much do I have". The bill screen answers the question "how much do I owe?". The caixinha screen should answer "how much can I spend?" — and currently it does not.
Hypothesis
The user does not associate the bill screen with available limit information. Not perceiving themselves as someone who "owes", they do not open the bill screen — and therefore never find the "Available limit R$60.00" field that is correctly displayed there. The information exists in the product, but is positioned in a mental context that is incompatible with the purchase decision moment.
None of the active participants reported consulting the bill screen to check the limit before purchasing. When verification happened, it was done via the caixinha — the screen that does not show the available limit.
Direct impact The correct information exists in the product but is inaccessible given the user's mental model. Editing the copy is not enough — the available limit needs to be brought to the caixinha screen.
📊Suggested BA analysis
Compare frequency of access to the Caixinha screen vs. the Bill screen among active ABCard customers. If the Caixinha is accessed far more frequently, it confirms it is the limit verification point — and that available limit information needs to be there.
💡 Follow-up user research
Which screen would you open to check how much you can spend on the card? Unmoderated survey with a simple routing task: show both screens (Caixinha and Bill) and ask where the user would look for the available limit before making a purchase.

Finding 3 — Success screens
A structurally misleading sentence in onboarding
The "Your limit is ready to use" screen contains:

This message is true only when the user pays with external funds (current account or bank slip). When paying with the caixinha itself — via autoseize or manual payment — the money does not come back: it was used to pay the bill. The limit is only replenished if a new deposit is made.

The sentence does not distinguish between the two scenarios. In onboarding — the moment of highest user attention — the product describes the money cycle in a way that may not match reality depending on how the user pays the bill. The quantitative data shows that 47% of active and 40% of inactive users pay the bill using the Caixinha (fully or partially) — meaning the misleading sentence affects nearly half of all users who pay the bill.

The relationship between this sentence and the mental models observed in the field is plausible, but not directly tested. The sentence describes a cycle — deposit, becomes limit, use, pay, comes back — without specifying that "comes back" presupposes payment with external funds. Nycole, who believed the Caixinha was untouchable by the bank, may have read this as a promise of automatic replenishment.
"I think when you put some [money in]... I do not know if that is what happens, if they wait for payment, right? If like R$2 comes in, will they take it or wait for me to actually pay." — Nycole (S2, inactive), signalling uncertainty and confusion about whether the bank automatically deducts the bill amount
Direct impact Nycole's uncertainty about whether the bank "would take" the money automatically — or whether she needs to pay — may be rooted in the success screen copy, which describes the payment cycle incompletely. Fixing this copy is a prerequisite for any onboarding or education intervention.

Communication
Recommendation 3
Replace static onboarding with progressive education and a proactive communication flow

The current model delivers all explanations at once at activation, and ends communication there. The field showed that real understanding comes from the experience of use — and that early frustration (expectation gap, declined purchases without explanation, money "disappearing" on the bill) filters out who continues before any learning consolidates. The problem is not just the onboarding: it is the absence of proactive communication throughout the entire journey.

96,2%decline rate among inactive users
5/6sessions with expectation gap
25,8%inactive users notice their declines — most accumulate in silence

One possible path: combine layered onboarding with a proactive communication flow, triggered by key journey events. The core explanation (what the limit is, what happens to the money after a purchase) should arrive when it is relevant — not all at once at activation. Examples of triggers: first decline → explain why it happened and how to avoid it; autoseize executed → notify that the Caixinha was used and the limit reset to zero; first billing cycle completed → confirm what happened to the balance and limit; low Caixinha balance → alert before a purchase is declined. The 96.2% decline rate among inactive users and 25.8% of declines noticed (against a real median of 4) suggests that frustration accumulates in silence — a proactive flow could intercept dropout before it consolidates.

Real-Time Education: If a purchase is declined and the minor has balance in their main account, the app can send an immediate push: "Your purchase did not go through because you only have R$10 in your Card Caixinha. Want to move your balance now and try again?"
💡 Idea 2
First Spend Gamification: Encourage a streak of 3 error-free purchases to "unlock" some benefit, countering dropout after the first decline.
Quali basis: Expectation gap in 5/6 sessions; Nicole (S2) — abandoned without knowing the reason for declines; Davi (S1) — 13 automatic declines unnoticed. Margin of error and confidence interval

The margin of error indicates how much the percentages observed in the sample may differ from the actual population values. A 95% confidence interval means that if the survey were repeated 100 times with different samples, in 95 of those repetitions the true result would fall within the calculated interval.

Considering a population of approximately 90,000 U18 ABCard customers and using the conservative proportion estimate (p = 50%, which maximises the margin):
  • U18 Sample (n = 700): margem de erro de ±3,7 p.p. com 95% de confiança. Ou seja, um percentual de 60% observado na amostra indica que o valor real na população está entre 56,3% e 63,7%.
  • LG Sample (n = 469): margin of error of ±4.5 p.p. at 95% confidence. A percentage of 60% indicates a true value between 55.5% and 64.5%.

Note: the margin applies to percentages calculated over the total sample. Smaller subgroups (e.g. non-converted n=140, encouragers n=73) have larger margins and should be interpreted with greater caution.

Why we use median instead of mean in this research

In this research, the behavioural variables — number of completed purchases, number of declines and amount deposited in the caixinha — show strongly skewed distributions, with a small number of very active users pulling the mean upward. In these cases, the median (the value of the user in the middle of the ordered distribution) represents the typical experience more faithfully than the mean (the sum divided by the total number of users).

Concrete example — declines among active users (n=294):

The median of 9 describes the middle user: half of active users have up to 9 declines, the other half have more.

For percentages and rates (decline rate, response distributions), the nature of the variable already eliminates the problem — and we report the direct value.
